THE LARCENY BREAK

A POST-PROP 47 ANALYTICAL REPORT // SFPD INDEX

For most of the 2000s, San Francisco's larceny incidents held remarkably flat. They hovered between 24,000 and 27,000 per year with almost no variation. Then came November 2014. Proposition 47 reclassified theft under $950 as a misdemeanor, and within three years the city was logging nearly 60,000 incidents annually. What the data shows isn't a gradual drift. It is a break.

CASE FILE 01: THE LINE CHART. Pre-2014, incidents held steady around 25,000/year for over a decade. By 2018, that number had hit ~60,000, which is a 140% increase in four years. The dashed line marks November 2014. Everything left of it looks like a different city.

The increase wasn't spread across the city equally. The Northern and Central districts, comprising the tourist corridors and the retail core, absorbed the sharpest climb. Meanwhile the outer residential neighborhoods like Sunset and Richmond barely registered the change. The $950 threshold didn't just redefine what was prosecutable. It apparently also redrew the map of where it was worth attempting

CASE FILE 02: THE CHOROPLETH (BEFORE vs. AFTER). Left panel shows 2003–2014; right shows 2015–2023. Northern and Central districts darken considerably post-Prop 47, absorbing incidents well above the 70k cumulative range. Outer residential districts remain in the light orange band throughout both periods.

Numbers can lie by omission. Larceny going up doesn't mean much on its own, unless you look at what it did to everything around it. In the sunburst below, each district gets a wedge, and each wedge breaks down into crime categories. Larceny Theft dominates every single outer ring, in every district, in both time periods. Burglary, vehicle theft, and vandalism are still there, but they are slivers. Larceny became the whole frame. To see the shift at the neighborhood level, click into a specific district and hover to compare the slivers of remaining property crime.

CASE FILE 03: THE SUNBURST. Inner ring = Before Prop 47 (2003–2014). Outer ring = After (2015–2023). Each wedge is a police district. The red band (Larceny Theft) overwhelms the outer ring across all districts post-2014. Burglary (blue) and Motor Vehicle Theft (green) remain proportionally stable, appearing as small slivers in both eras.

ANALYTICAL CONTEXT

Data can be misleading The SFPD introduced online and mobile reporting tools around the same period. Suddenly, filing a report for a smashed car window took two minutes on a phone instead of a trip to the station. Some of this wave may simply be crimes that always existed but were never counted.

The geography tells a second story. The surge was hyper-concentrated in districts with the highest density of tourists and high-value tech commuters. As the market for stolen electronics moved to the internet, the reward for a quick break-in grew.

The 2020 dip is almost like a controlled experiment. COVID emptied the commercial corridors that generated most of the incidents. The targets disappeared, and the numbers crashed instantly. The rebound after 2021 suggests the underlying conditions hadn't gone away.
