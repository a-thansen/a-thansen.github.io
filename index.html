THE WAVE

San Francisco Larceny // 2003—2023

Beneath the shimmering fog of the Golden Gate, a different kind of mist began to settle over San Francisco in the early 2010s. It wasn't atmospheric; it was criminal. For years, the city’s property crime rates moved with a predictable, rhythmic hum—until a single legislative pivot in November 2014 changed the frequency forever.

Proposition 47, billed as the "Safe Neighborhoods and Schools Act," was intended to end mass incarceration for non-violent offenses. By reclassifying "non-serious" property crimes under $950 as misdemeanors, it effectively shifted the legal gravity of shoplifting and car break-ins. What followed was a decoupling of San Francisco’s larceny rates from almost every other major crime metric in the city.

THE DETECTIVE'S NOTE: A REASONABLE DOUBT

Data, like any witness, can be coached. While the visual evidence of Prop 47's impact is staggering, we must account for the "Reporting Paradox." During the peak of this wave, the SFPD introduced digital reporting tools, making it easier than ever to file a report for a smashed window from a smartphone.

Is the wave real? Or is it a reflection of a city that finally found a way to count the crimes it had been ignoring for decades? The truth likely lies somewhere in the overlap of tech-driven tourism, widening inequality, and a legal system that redefined the price of a crime.
