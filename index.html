The Larceny Wave

Exploring the surge of property crime in San Francisco, the impact of Proposition 47, and the data behind the headlines.

San Francisco has long grappled with a reputation for property crime, but the data reveals a story much more complex than the headlines suggest. Utilizing the San Francisco Police Department (SFPD) Incident Reports dataset, which spans from 2003 to the present, we can trace the distinct patterns of "Larceny/Theft"—a category primarily driven by auto glass break-ins and retail shoplifting. But has it always been this way?

A pivotal moment in this timeline is November 2014, when California voters passed Proposition 47. This measure reclassified certain non-violent property crimes under $950 from felonies to misdemeanors. The immediate question for data analysts and city residents alike is: how did the streets of San Francisco react to this legislative shift? As the data below illustrates, the aftermath was stark and immediate.

Figure 1: The Inflection Point. Total Larceny Incidents by Year. Notice the immediate and dramatic shift following the passage of Prop 47 in late 2014, breaking a decade-long plateau.

The post-2014 surge is undeniable, eventually peaking into a massive wave in the late 2010s. However, a city-wide line chart often masks neighborhood-level realities. Did this wave of larceny wash over the entire city equally, or was it intensely localized?

To answer this, we can aggregate the data by Police District to compare the landscape before Proposition 47 against the years that followed. The resulting choropleth maps reveal a striking concentration. Rather than a uniform increase across all residential districts, the "heat" of the larceny wave was overwhelmingly absorbed by the downtown core, commercial hubs, and major tourist corridors.

Figure 2: The Epicenter. A side-by-side choropleth comparison of Larceny counts by Police District before 2014 (Left) and after 2014 (Right). Darker reds indicate higher concentrations of incidents.

With larceny concentrated so heavily in specific zones, a critical thinker must ask: was San Francisco simply experiencing a broader crime wave? Did all crimes surge in tandem, or was this phenomenon unique to larceny?

By comparing larceny against a broader spectrum of offenses—including burglary, motor vehicle theft, assault, vandalism, and drug offenses—a fascinating divergence emerges. Correlation vs. Causation

It is tempting to look at the sharp increase in larceny following 2014 and conclude that Proposition 47 is the sole culprit. However, in data science, correlation does not automatically equal causation. During this exact same period, the SFPD introduced a streamlined online reporting system for car break-ins. Because victims no longer had to wait for an officer to file a report, the "surge" in the data is partially driven by an increase in reporting, not just an increase in actual crime.

Furthermore, this era coincided with a massive tech boom, widening wealth inequality, and a historic surge in tourism—creating a target-rich environment for auto-glass theft. While Proposition 47 is a compelling piece of the narrative, the real-world data is deeply interwoven with these complex social, technological, and economic dynamics.
