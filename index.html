THE WAVE

SAN FRANCISCO LARCENY // REPORT 2.0

Before 2014, larceny in San Francisco followed a predictable, almost rhythmic hum. It was a background noise of city life—car windows shattered here and there, a shoplifted coat there. But in November of that year, a single legislative pivot, **Proposition 47**, altered the city’s criminal frequency forever.

By reclassifying non-violent thefts under $950 from felonies to misdemeanors, the law intended to reduce prison overcrowding. Instead, it seems to have acted as a chemical catalyst. In the data that followed, "Larceny" decoupled from every other crime metric in the city, rising with a violence that earned it a new name among locals and data scientists alike: **The Wave.**

LOG 01: THE INFLECTION. The timeline shows a decade of stability shattered. From a baseline of roughly 25,000 incidents, the city saw a vertical climb post-2014, reaching a peak of nearly 60,000 reports in 2017. The sharp valley in 2020 marks the only time the wave broke—a direct result of the global pandemic.

But a city-wide average is a mask. To see the true face of the wave, you have to look at the streets. This wasn't a rising tide that lifted all boats; it was a targeted flood. While quiet districts like Taraval and Park remained relatively stable, the commercial heart of the city began to bleed red.

The geography of the surge tells a story of opportunity. Larceny didn't just increase; it migrated. It swarmed the tourist corridors and high-density retail hubs where the $950 boundary provided a new, invisible calculus of risk for offenders.

LOG 02: THE EPICENTER. The shift from Left (Pre-2014) to Right (Post-2014) is visceral. The Southern and Central districts transform into deep-red hotspots, absorbing the overwhelming majority of the city's new larceny volume.

To understand the "Takeover," we have to look at the criminal makeup of the city. Did burglars simply switch to car break-ins? Or did larceny simply eat the rest of the property crime landscape? The Sunburst below visualizes the "Genetic Makeup" of San Francisco’s crime before and after the shift.

When you drill down into the "After" era, the red wedge for Larceny doesn't just grow—it dominates. It indicates a fundamental shift in the city's criminal identity: from a balanced mix of property crimes to a city primarily defined by a single, overwhelming category of theft.

THE DETECTIVE'S NOTE: A REASONABLE DOUBT

Data, like any witness, can be coached. While the visual evidence of a post-Prop 47 surge is staggering, a critical thinker must account for the **Reporting Paradox**. During the peak of this wave, the SFPD introduced digital reporting tools, allowing victims to file a report for a smashed window from their smartphone in seconds.

Is the Wave real? Or is it a reflection of a city that finally found a way to count the crimes it had been ignoring for decades? The truth likely lies in the middle: a combination of easier reporting, a massive tech-driven wealth gap, and a legal system that recalibrated the price of a stolen bag.
